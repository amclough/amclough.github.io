SafePaths Design Study

The Product: Begun in the MIT Media Lab and eventually spun into a global nonprofit organization, Safe Paths was the first major COVID contact tracing app in the United States. The original app, featured here, relied on GPS technology. Since then, we've integrated with the Google Apple Exposure Notification (GAEN) protocol to deploy Bluetooth-based contact tracing technology in countries all over the world.


What: Privacy-First COVID Contact Tracing Solution

Role: Founding UIUX Lead

Design team: A globally distributed network of over 100 volunteer designers. Special thanks to Jared Snider, Bill Fallon, as well as Tito Bottita and his team at Upstatement

Discovery Phase

Our initial team began development with a white paper on all the things that had gone wrong with digital contact tracing.

China's App Integrated into Alipay

China's app gave users a red or green "go, no-go" pass based on location logs.


Problem: overly simple UI with too little context confused users

“These big data platforms have made me really mad,” wrote Weibo user Miuyekanshijie on March 23. “My health code error remains unresolved … it’s seriously affecting my ability to travel.” - Japan Times

South Korea's "COVID 100m" App

COVID 100m publicized location logs, showing "hot spots" where COVID patients had been.


Problem: too much data visibility compromises privacy and puts individuals at risk

"[Some of my patients] were more afraid of being blamed than dying of the virus” -Lee Su-young, Psychiatrist at Myongji Hospital, South Korea

Fostering trust in digital contact tracing

Data Privacy Emphasis on Choice

Low user adoption, often driven by privacy concerns, is the Achilles heel for digital contact tracing. We made conscious content decisions to emphasize choice and provide data transparency through the onboarding flow.

Functional Transparency

A major risk we identified through discovery was the potential for users to interpret our "No Contact" screen as an all-clear. Clear references to "No Known Contact" and "Based on Available Data," with explanations about the public record GPS reporting process helped communicate transparency around what we were - and weren't - telling users.

Deferring to Public Health Authorities

Misinformation around COVID was (and is) rampant, and guidance from health authorities changes rapidly with scientific developments. We kept our partners front and center in our content design, linking to primary sources whenever possible.

Staying culturally competent

In addition to the Google Apple Exposure Notification (GAEN) guidelines, our final product ended up white labeled to allow for customization and better cultural relevance for jurisdictions. The MIT/Harvard name brand that gave us legitimacy in the U.S. undermined our authority in many developing countries. Similarly, language nuances and each jurisdiction's varying infection states weren't something our organization could handle. A few of the deployments so far include Guam, Cyprus, Hawaii, and Minnesota.
